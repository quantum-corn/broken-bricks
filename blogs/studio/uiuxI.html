What is UI/UX?

As someone just dipping their toes into the vast ocean of UI/UX, my understanding is still quite basic—but here’s what I’ve grasped so far. UI, or User Interface, and UX, or User Experience, are often spoken of together, yet they represent distinct facets of design.

If you’re already reading this, you likely know what those acronyms stand for, but just to recap: UI focuses on the aesthetics and functionality of digital interfaces, making them not only beautiful but also intuitive and user-friendly. Picture a bird: while a coder might be responsible for crafting the bird’s wings, it’s the UI/UX designer who decides how those wings will look, how they’ll be decorated, and even how the bird might interact with and use them. Your design toolkit? Pure imagination, with coders bringing your visions to life. From websites and apps to logos and games, the magic of design is at your fingertips. But don’t be fooled—designing isn’t just about creativity. It’s a meticulous process involving research, planning, and execution.

When clients present you with a product—be it a website, app, or something else—they come with a wishlist of features. Your job as a UI/UX designer is to seamlessly integrate these features into a cohesive and engaging design. Everything from color schemes to the placement of buttons and icons falls under your purview. The challenge? Creating a design that not only meets the client’s needs but also resonates with users.

So far, this is my fledgling understanding of UI/UX—a mere glimpse into what is, in reality, a vast and intricate field. UI may seem more straightforward than UX, which delves into deeper, more complex principles. I haven’t yet fully explored UX, so I’ll save my thoughts on that for a future chapter, once I’ve gotten a firmer grasp.

Why did I choose to learn UI/UX?

This is a question I’ve been asking myself a lot lately, especially as someone transitioning from the arts into tech. My academic journey began in humanities, leading me to pursue a bachelor’s in English Literature and now a master’s. But after spending years immersed in academia, I’ve come to realize that it might not offer me the fulfillment I’m seeking in life. So, I decided to pivot.

Now, if I were to go by market trends, learning to code would have been the obvious choice—lucrative, in-demand, and prestigious. But after a brief flirtation with coding, I quickly realized it wasn’t for me. My creative, somewhat irrational mind struggled with the logical structure required for coding. And so, I turned to UI/UX—a field that allows me to be part of the ever-evolving tech world while still indulging my love for creativity.

UI/UX is a domain where you can gain a solid understanding and practical skills in just a few months. Plus, I’ve always had an eye for design. Every time I browse a website or use an app, I find myself thinking, “I could design this better.” But translating that vision onto a screen is a whole different challenge, especially with my limited technical background. There’s a learning curve, particularly in grasping design proportions, but I’m eager to tackle it.

But let’s not romanticize this—it’s hard work. UI/UX might seem fun, but it’s a mentally demanding field. If you’re considering this path, persistence is your best friend. The initial allure of a good salary and creative freedom can quickly fade if you’re not genuinely invested in the work. You need to be sure this is what you want before you dive in. Once you’ve made that commitment, the journey begins. You’ll eventually learn how to navigate the waters, but you also need to figure out how to apply what you’ve learned effectively.

Dedication to learning is key—go deep, stay curious, and most importantly, keep practicing. Remember, you’re in a competitive field, so constant improvement is essential. And one last thing—learning never really ends, so stay hungry for knowledge.

The Kickoff!

Driven by my boundless curiosity, I decided to jump straight into the world of design, starting with Figma. It’s arguably the best tool out there for graphic design, but like anything worthwhile, it comes with its own learning curve. There are countless YouTube tutorials available, but I opted to dive in headfirst. I haven’t tried any of those videos, and just started raw, so if I find something good, I’ll be sharing them here in my blog. Now I don’t really suggest you jump into this quicksand without understanding and experience, but if you want to, who’s even stopping?

Here’s my first attempt at a design in Figma. It’s more of a poster than a webpage, and I still have a lot to learn—especially when it comes to prototyping, which is a crucial aspect of Figma. Another essential feature of Figma is its plugins. Figma is powerful on its own, but plugins elevate it to another level, expanding what you can achieve.

https://uxplanet.org/20-figma-plugins-every-designer-must-have-9bd6f1733780

Now all that is left to do is, download your required plugin and try it on your project.

Now that you’ve got your plugins, it’s time to start designing. My suggestion? Begin with a desktop blog site. Keep proportions in mind, and don’t hesitate—just dive in and start creating. The more you practice, the better you’ll get. Here are a few steps to keep in mind:

  • Have a plan. Before you start, visualize the design in your mind. If you don’t have a tablet, grab a pen and paper and sketch out your ideas. Design the base of your blog site.
  • Define Features and Elements.  Write down what features and elements you want to put in your blog. You can’t put everything that you have in your mind. Simplicity is key—don’t overcrowd your design. Focus on the essentials, and remember, users appreciate clean, functional designs.
  • Implement Thoughtfully. As you add elements to your design, ensure everything is placed intuitively and elegantly. Users shouldn’t have to struggle to find what they need. Aim for a balance between beauty and usability. Nobody likes messy websites or too many things to distract them.
  • Create a Flowchart.  Once your design is ready, map out the user journey by designing the pages that will follow each button click.

You are ready to go now. Happy designing!
